ideal parents

a quiet introduction to the Ideal Parent Figure protocol, a visualization practice from attachment psychology, explained plainly.

what this is

Some of us grew up without enough of certain things: protection when the world felt dangerous, an adult who really saw us, comfort that arrived when we needed it, someone whose face lit up when we walked in, a steady hand at our back as we went out to explore. The absence leaves a quiet imprint, a way of expecting the world and other people to be.

The Ideal Parent Figure protocol is a practice developed by the psychologists Daniel P. Brown and David Elliott. The move at its heart is simple and a little strange: you imagine, in a relaxed state, parents who are made exactly for you. Invented ones, deliberately different from the parents you actually had and from anyone you know. The past is left as it was.

What the practice works with is the imprint. The body responds to vividly imagined experience, and feeling the scenes matters more than believing them. With repetition, the experience of being protected, seen, soothed, delighted in, and encouraged gives the nervous system a second pattern to draw on. People who practice describe the old expectations slowly getting company: a felt sense that safety and care are possible.

This page promises nothing. It only tries to explain the practice clearly, because the original material is long and clinical and easy to bounce off. What follows is an interpretation, one way of seeing it, offered in the hope that it helps.

the practice, in one breath

Settle somewhere comfortable and let yourself get quiet. Bring to mind yourself as a young child, three or four years old perhaps. Then let imagination supply two parents who are ideally suited to that child: their presence, their warmth, the way they are simply there. You don't have to construct them carefully. Let them appear, and notice what it feels like in your body to be a child in their care.

That's the whole shape of it. The substance is in the qualities these figures embody: five conditions that attachment research suggests a developing nervous system needs. Each one is a world of its own. Someone practicing might visualize scenes like the ones below.

the first condition

protection

Before anything else, a child needs to feel that someone stronger is between them and the dangerous parts of the world. Ideal parents are unmistakably protective: they notice what threatens you before you do, and nothing gets past them. Their protection is calm, like the canopy of a large tree. Things may rain down; they don't reach you.

“We've got this. Nothing can get to you while we're here. And we're here.”

You might picture: dusk, weather moving in the distance, and the complete bodily ease of a child who doesn't have to keep watch, because someone else is keeping it.

the second condition

attunement

Attunement is being accurately seen. An attuned parent tracks what you feel, often before you can name it, and gets down to your eye level to meet it. When you bring them something small, a leaf or a half-formed worry, they receive it as exactly as important as it is to you. Nothing about you is too minor to be noticed, and nothing is too much.

“Show me. I want to understand it the way you see it.”

You might picture: a garden, a found treasure, and an adult whose whole attention has knelt down to meet yours. The feeling of being known from the inside.

the third condition

soothing

Distress always comes. When it does, a child needs comfort that actually arrives and actually works. Ideal parents are easy to reach and impossible to exhaust. Their soothing slows your breathing; they don't need you to feel better quickly. Big feelings get to be their full size, and they stay until the feelings pass through.

“You don't have to hold this by yourself. Stay as long as you like.”

You might picture: evening, rain on the window, a blanket, and the particular heaviness of a small body that has stopped bracing.

the fourth condition

expressed delight

This one is easy to miss, and its absence is easy to carry for decades: a child needs people who are visibly glad that they exist. Delight is different from praise. Praise responds to what you do; delight responds to the fact of you. Ideal parents light up when you come into the room. You can see it on their faces, and you don't have to earn it first.

“There you are. You make this house brighter just by being in it.”

You might picture: morning light, a twirl, a crayon drawing held up high, and two faces that were already lit before they saw the drawing.

the fifth condition

support for exploration

The first four conditions build a secure base. The fifth is what the base is for: leaving it. Ideal parents are genuinely enthusiastic about you going out into the world, trying things, becoming whoever you turn out to be. They don't need you to stay close to feel okay, and they don't need you to succeed to be proud. Their confidence in you arrives before your own does, and you can borrow it.

“Go see. We'll be right here when you look back, and right here when you come home.”

You might picture: a hill, a wide horizon, your own arms out for balance, and behind you, close enough to hear and far enough to let you climb, two figures who are certain you can.

returning

The protocol is gentle, and it is repetition that lets it work. A few quiet minutes, often, matters more than a long session once. One small everyday shape:

  1. Settle, soften, and bring to mind your young self.
  2. Let the ideal parents appear, or just one of the five conditions, whichever you most needed today.
  3. Stay where the feeling is. If a scene feels warm in the body, linger there; let it get one shade more vivid.
  4. Before opening your eyes, notice the felt sense and carry a trace of it into the day.

The figures will change between sittings. That's fine; what you are revisiting is the feeling of being in their care, and that survives any change of face. Over time, many people notice the practice continuing on its own: a moment of stress in adult life, and something in them supplies the response a good parent would have had.

a word of care

Protection applies to this page too. For many people, this practice is a gentle, self-guided thing. For some, especially where early life involved frightening caregivers or where parental imagery itself stirs up a lot, the practice can bring intense material to the surface. If imagining parent figures floods you rather than settles you, that is meaningful information and a place to be gentle with yourself. The protocol was designed to be done with a trained guide, and doing it that way is the stronger version of the practice.

If what comes up feels like more than you want to hold alone, an attachment-focused therapist (the protocol comes from Brown & Elliott's "Three Pillars" approach) is the right company for it.
